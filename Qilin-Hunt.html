1. Executive Summary

Qilin, also known as Agenda and Water Galura in vendor reporting, is a financially motivated Ransomware-as-a-Service operation active since 2022. The group and its affiliates conduct double extortion: they steal data, pressure victims through leak portals, and encrypt Windows, Linux, and VMware ESXi environments when the intrusion reaches impact. This pack emphasizes precursor detection because the most reliable defender wins happen before exfiltration or encryption.

Primary-source Red Canary facts: Red Canary's 2026 ransomware trend page says it observed Qilin-related data exfiltration or encryption activity in 2025; observed Play, Qilin, and Akira intrusions where affiliates exploited Veeam CVE-2023-27532 and CVE-2024-40711; and, in CVE-2024-40711 cases, adversaries used Veeam.Backup.MountService.exe to spawn cmd.exe and add a local admin named Admon. Red Canary also highlights ransomware precursor detection across initial access, discovery, lateral movement, defense evasion, C2, RMM abuse, shadow-copy deletion, and domain-trust discovery.
Enrichment / adjacent pivots: Sophos reported Qilin GPO abuse to run IPScanner.ps1 and harvest Chrome credentials, then deploy run.bat; a separate Sophos MSP incident involved Evilginx-style ScreenConnect phishing, malicious ru.msi, Veeam credential-theft tooling, WinRAR staging, EasyUpload exfiltration, and Qilin deployment. Trend Micro maps Qilin/Agenda to Rust and Linux/ESXi variants, PsExec, WinSCP, BYOVD, Veeam database credential access, MEGAsync/WinSCP/s5cmd exfiltration, and service/snapshot disruption. Microsoft describes Linux/ESXi Qilin behavior that disables VMware and backup services, uses BYOVD, and drops README-RECOVER-style notes.
Supplied source context: Cybernews reported on May 6, 2026 that Qilin claimed Sysco on its leak site and posted alleged documents. Treat this as a media-reported leak-site claim until corroborated by victim or incident-response reporting; use it for sector urgency, not for technical IOCs.

11. Hardening

Immediate - 0 to 72 hours

  • Patch or isolate Veeam Backup & Replication systems exposed to CVE-2023-27532 and CVE-2024-40711 paths; confirm no internet exposure to Veeam management/service ports.
  • Audit Veeam service accounts, stored credentials, repositories, hypervisor credentials, and cloud credentials; rotate if exploitation is suspected.
  • Block exact known-bad domains/hashes and high-confidence campaign paths in Falcon, DNS, proxy, and EDR controls.
  • Restrict RMM tools to approved vendors, tenants, certificates, and egress destinations. Block unapproved Quick Assist/AnyDesk/SimpleHelp/ScreenConnect/Splashtop usage.
  • Enforce phishing-resistant MFA for VPN, RMM, Veeam portals, IdP admin, and helpdesk/admin workflows.
  • Protect immutable backups and verify at least one restore path that does not depend on domain credentials.

Near term - 2 to 4 weeks

  • Move backup management to a dedicated segmented admin network with hardened jump hosts and no broad internet egress.
  • Require approval and CODEOWNERS-style review for GPOs that create scheduled tasks, run scripts, or touch browser/credential paths.
  • Deploy ASR rules and tamper protection for credential theft, script abuse, and shadow-copy deletion where compatible.
  • Baseline ESXi/vCenter admin commands and alert on snapshot deletion, VM kill, SSH enablement, or bulk power actions outside maintenance windows.
  • Disable browser password storage via policy and migrate users to managed password vaults with conditional access.
  • Create RMM tenant inventory and alert on new RMM installs, rogue tenants, or unmanaged-device admin logins.

Strategic - 30 to 90 days

  • Adopt privileged access workstations for backup, hypervisor, identity, and RMM administration.
  • Implement network egress allow-listing for servers and backup systems; block direct outbound file-sharing destinations by default.
  • Build cross-plane ransomware precursor correlation: Veeam exploit, new admin, RMM login, discovery, archive staging, and backup disruption.
  • Test restore procedures quarterly with domain compromise and hypervisor compromise assumptions.
  • Codify ransomware tabletop roles for SOC, EDR, infrastructure, legal, privacy, communications, and executive leadership.
